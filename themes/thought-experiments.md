# Theme: Thought Experiments

Physics and philosophy thought experiments that changed how we understand reality —
designed as shirts that invite "wait, explain that" reactions.

## Why this fits the shop

A thought experiment is the purest form of the shop's spirit: a real, checkable idea
with zero equipment required. Each one is a question that seems absurd until you take it
seriously, at which point it dissolves something you thought you understood. The visual
challenge is translating a pure concept into a diagram — which is exactly what physicists
and philosophers have been doing since the 1800s.

All of the concepts below are out of copyright. The risk is only in the specific
illustrations used — all visuals must be original or PD.

## Sub-ideas

### 1. Schrödinger's cat (1935)

**Original source:** Erwin Schrödinger, "Die gegenwärtige Situation in der
Quantenmechanik" (*Naturwissenschaften*, 23(48), 807–812, 1935). Article PD in
most jurisdictions (author died 1961; EU PD 70 years after death = 2031; US PD:
published 1935, pre-1978, no renewal found — likely PD in US). The *concept* is not
copyrightable anywhere. The famous "cat in a box" diagram is a generic concept illustration;
any original rendering is safe.

**The setup:** A cat in a sealed box with a radioactive atom, a Geiger counter, and a
flask of poison. Until observed, quantum mechanics says the atom is both decayed and
not — so the cat is, until observed, both alive and dead. Schrödinger invented this to
*mock* the Copenhagen interpretation, not to endorse it.

**Visual direction (original diagram, no third-party IP needed):**
- Box (cross-section view): cat silhouette on the left, apparatus on the right (Geiger
  tube, flask, atom symbol).
- Wavefunction label: `|ψ⟩ = (1/√2)(|alive⟩ + |dead⟩)`
- Status label above the box: "ALIVE / DEAD" or use a superposition bracket.
- Clean, minimalist — works as white line art on dark or black on white.

**Slogan options:**
- *"Until you look, both."*
- *"|ψ⟩ = (1/√2)(|alive⟩ + |dead⟩)"* — equation only, no label
- *"Schrödinger's cat, 1935. Not an endorsement."*

**License:** Original diagram — no third-party IP. Schrödinger's text is PD in US;
EU still under copyright until 2031 (use concept only, not translated text).

**Source:** Schrödinger (1935), Naturwissenschaften 23, 807–812 (accessed via published
record; concept is standard physics knowledge).

### 2. Maxwell's demon (1871)

**Original source:** James Clerk Maxwell, *Theory of Heat* (London: Longmans, Green,
1871) — pre-1927, PD worldwide. Maxwell introduced the demon in a letter to P.G. Tait
(11 Dec 1867) and published it in *Theory of Heat*. The concept: a tiny demon guards a
trapdoor between two gas chambers and sorts fast-moving molecules (hot) into one side
and slow ones (cold) into the other — apparently violating the second law of
thermodynamics without doing work. Resolution (Szilard 1929, Landauer 1961): information
erasure has a thermodynamic cost.

**Visual direction (original):**
- Two chambers side by side, one warm (red tones) one cool (blue).
- Demon (cartoon imp or stylised figure) at the trapdoor.
- Molecules as circles with velocity arrows — larger/faster on the warm side.
- Labels: "HOT" / "COLD", ΔS = 0? crossed out, with small note: "Landauer's principle, 1961."

**Slogan options:**
- *"The demon that almost broke entropy."*
- *"Maxwell's demon, 1867. Resolution: 1961."*
- *"Information is physical."* (Landauer's conclusion)

**License:** Maxwell's text is PD. Original diagram is agent-generated; no third-party
IP. Landauer's 1961 paper (IBM J. Res. Dev., 5(3), 183–191) — concept PD, cite as source.

**Source:** Maxwell (1871), *Theory of Heat*, Chapter 22. Szilard (1929), Z. Phys. 53,
840–856. Landauer (1961), IBM J. Res. Dev. 5(3), 183–191.

### 3. Einstein's elevator (1907)

**Original source:** Albert Einstein, "Über das Relativitätsprinzip und die aus demselben
gezogenen Folgerungen" (*Jahrbuch der Radioaktivität und Elektronik*, 4, 411–462, 1907) —
pre-1927, PD worldwide. This is the thought experiment that gave Einstein the equivalence
principle: a person inside a closed elevator cannot distinguish between a gravitational
field and uniform acceleration. It is the seed of General Relativity.

**Visual direction (original):**
- Two side-by-side panels, same elevator cross-section.
- Left: elevator in deep space with upward acceleration arrow (rocket below optional);
  person inside presses feet to floor. Label: "a = g upward acceleration."
- Right: elevator stationary on Earth's surface; same person presses feet to floor. Label:
  "g = 9.8 m/s²."
- Central connector: "=" or "≡ (locally indistinguishable)"
- Optional: person dropping a ball — it falls identically in both panels.

**Slogan options:**
- *"Locally indistinguishable."*
- *"The equivalence principle, 1907."*
- *"Free fall is the absence of gravity."*

**License:** Einstein's text is PD. Original diagram — no IP concerns.

**Source:** Einstein (1907), Jahrbuch der Radioaktivität 4, 411–462. Standard general
relativity reference: Misner, Thorne & Wheeler, *Gravitation* (1973), §1.3 (for the
modern formulation — the concept is PD, cite for context only).

### 4. Laplace's demon (1814)

**Original source:** Pierre-Simon Laplace, *Essai philosophique sur les probabilités*
(Paris: Courcier, 1814) — pre-1927, PD worldwide. The famous passage: *"An intellect
which at a certain moment would know all forces that set nature in motion, and all
positions of all items of which nature is composed, if this intellect were also vast
enough to submit these data to analysis, it would embrace in a single formula the
movements of the greatest bodies of the universe and those of the tiniest atom; for such
an intellect nothing would be uncertain and the future just like the past would be present
before its eyes."* Laplace's word for this intellect: *"une intelligence"* (not "demon" —
that term came later from commentators).

**The idea:** Perfect knowledge of all particles + laws of nature → perfect prediction.
The clockwork universe. Broken by: quantum indeterminacy (Heisenberg 1927) and chaos
theory (Lorenz 1963).

**Visual direction (original):**
- A large gear-and-clockwork diagram (homage to the clockwork-universe metaphor),
  centred on a stylised "all-knowing eye" or brain.
- Below: the Laplace quote (short form), attributed and dated.
- Optional second panel: crossed-out gear replaced by ψ|Ψ⟩ (quantum uncertainty) and
  a butterfly (chaos / Lorenz attractor).

**Slogan options:**
- *"An intellect vast enough…"* + year 1814
- *"The universe as equation. (Broken 1927.)"*
- *"Laplace's demon. Heisenberg's ghost."*

**License:** Laplace's text is PD (pre-1927). The quote is PD. Original diagram — no IP.

**Source:** Laplace (1814), *Essai philosophique sur les probabilités*, Introduction
(translation by F.W. Truscott & F.L. Emory, 1902, PD). Heisenberg (1927), Z. Phys. 43,
172–198. Lorenz (1963), J. Atmos. Sci. 20(2), 130–141.

### 5. Fermi paradox (1950)

**Original source:** Enrico Fermi, 1950 — the famous Los Alamos lunch conversation.
Fermi asked: "Where is everybody?" referring to extraterrestrial civilisations. The
question has never been formally published by Fermi himself; the original account is by
Eric Jones (1985), LA-10311-MS, Los Alamos National Laboratory — LANL technical reports
are US government works, PD on publication.

**The idea:** The galaxy is ~13.6 billion years old. If interstellar travel is possible,
even at 0.1% of light speed, a civilisation could colonise the entire galaxy in
~100 million years — 1/100 of the galaxy's age. Yet we detect nothing. The silence is
the question.

**Visual direction (original):**
- A deep star-field background (use a night-sky photo — NASA images are PD; or generate
  procedurally using Matplotlib/NumPy scatter with varied star brightness).
- Central text: *"WHERE IS EVERYBODY?"* large, white, in a clean sans-serif.
- Below: Fermi, Los Alamos, 1950.
- Optional: a small data table showing estimated number of stars in Milky Way (2×10¹¹),
  fraction with planets, fraction possibly habitable — all accepted estimates, citable.

**Slogan options:**
- *"WHERE IS EVERYBODY?"* — stark, no context needed for the nerd buyer
- *"The Fermi Paradox. Still unanswered."*
- *"Galaxy age: 13.6 Gyr. Colonisation time: ~0.1 Gyr. Detections: 0."*

**License:** Fermi's 1950 question is not a published work; the concept is PD. NASA
star-field photos are PD. Jones (1985) LA-10311-MS is a US government work, PD.
Procedurally generated star field is entirely original.

**Source:** Jones, E.M. (1985), "Where is everybody? An account of Fermi's question,"
LA-10311-MS, Los Alamos National Laboratory (accessed via LANL technical reports, PD).
NASA Hubble Heritage images (NASA, ESA — public domain).

## Visual direction (series-wide)

- **Tone:** Serious-scientific, not jokey. Each diagram communicates a genuine idea.
  The wit comes from knowing what the idea means, not from the presentation.
- **Format:** Two-panel comparisons (Einstein's elevator), single-diagram with equation
  block (Schrödinger, Maxwell), quote-with-diagram (Laplace), text-dominant (Fermi).
- **Palette:** Dark background + white line art for a "chalkboard" look, or white
  background + dark line art for a "textbook" look. Both work for POD.
- **Automation:** All diagrams are original — producible with Matplotlib or the existing
  `scripts/eq_render.py`. No third-party images required.

## Shop fit

**High.** Thought experiments are:
- Physics-background-owner-friendly — all concepts are correct and verifiable
- Unique conversation starters; not the same as generic "science" merchandise
- Zero IP risk when diagrams are original
- Each has a natural companion product (poster / mug) for the same design

**Risk:** Schrödinger's cat is *extremely* crowded — hundreds of products on Redbubble,
TePublic, etc. The differentiator must be the equation (`|ψ⟩ = ...`) and the serious
tone ("not an endorsement"), not just cat + box. The other four (especially Laplace's
demon and the Fermi paradox text treatment) are significantly less saturated.

**Recommended launch:** Fermi paradox text-treatment (lowest art cost, most striking at
a glance) and Laplace's demon diagram (richest concept, least crowded niche).
