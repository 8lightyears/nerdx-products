# Theme: Non-Trivial Historical Curiosities

> Shop fit: shirts that tell a single true story no one else is telling.
> Each design is one surprising fact + the artefact or diagram that proves it.

---

## Scope and rationale

The defining feature of this group is density: one shirt, one verifiable story,
zero padding. The buyer learns something in the first five seconds. The shop's
physics-owner background is an asset here — curiosities with engineering or
scientific angles are especially credible and credibly explained.

All items in this group use original diagram/typographic layouts or PD source
material. None requires a third-party license unless explicitly noted.

## Sub-ideas

### 1. Silphium — the plant that went extinct for being too good

**The hook.** Silphium was an ancient North African plant so prized that it
appeared on Cyrenean coins and was valued weight-for-weight with silver. It was
used as a condiment, a medicine, and an early contraceptive. Overharvested by
the Roman empire, it went extinct sometime in the 1st century AD — the only
known plant to be deliberately consumed to extinction. No confirmed specimen
survives; only numismatic and textual records remain.

**Visual direction.**
- Option A: the Cyrenean silver tetradrachm circa 480–435 BC shows the silphium
  plant in profile — a clean plant illustration PD by age.
- Option B: typographic shirt: the plant name in Greek (σίλφιον) with the
  botanical classification used in ancient texts and the coin denominations.
- Option C: "Last known stock: approx. 50 AD" — mock expiry label over a
  stylised botanical drawing.

**License.** Coin illustrations from ancient sources and museum line drawings
are PD by age. Original typographic or botanical interpretations require no
license.

**Sources.**
- Pliny the Elder, *Naturalis Historia* Book XIX, ch. 15 (written c. AD 77–79,
  PD by age). Online: Perseus Digital Library.
- Brothwell, D. & Brothwell, P. *Food in Antiquity* (1969), pp. 106–107.

**Recommended launch.** Option C mock-expiry label — instantly readable, no
complex illustration required.

### 2. Antikythera mechanism — the oldest known analogue computer

**The hook.** Around 1900, sponge divers found a corroded lump in a 2,000-year-old
shipwreck off the Greek island of Antikythera. Decades of X-ray analysis revealed
a geared bronze device of extraordinary complexity: differential gears, pin-and-slot
couplings, and at least 37 interlocking wheels computing the position of the Sun and
Moon, eclipses on an 18-year Saros cycle, and the 4-year Olympiad calendar.
Nothing mechanically comparable appears in the historical record until the 14th
century AD — a gap of over 1,400 years.

**Visual direction.**
- Fragment X-ray reconstruction (available from the Antikythera Mechanism Research
  Project papers) shows the gear train; the shape is visually distinctive.
- Best option: original gear-train diagram derived from the research-paper schematics
  (de Solla Price, *Gears from the Greeks*, 1974, DOI 10.2307/3101118 — now PD in
  most jurisdictions). Draw as a clean line-art schematic.
- Caption: "Antikythera, c. 100 BC — 1,400 years ahead of schedule."

**License.** The mechanism itself is an ancient artefact. The Athens National
Archaeological Museum holds the fragments; their photographs are NOT freely usable.
Use only self-drawn gear schematics derived from the published research descriptions.
The de Solla Price 1974 paper diagrams are now old enough to be PD in most
jurisdictions, but safer to redraw from scratch. NASA 3D scan data (2016,
Freeth & Jones group) is published in academic papers; redraw from the description.

**Sources.**
- Price, D.J. de Solla. "Gears from the Greeks." *Transactions of the American
  Philosophical Society* 64:7 (1974). DOI 10.2307/3101118.
- Freeth, T. et al. "Decoding the ancient Greek astronomical calculator known as
  the Antikythera Mechanism." *Nature* 444 (2006), pp. 587–591.
  DOI 10.1038/nature05357.
- Freeth, T. & Jones, A. "The Cosmos in the Antikythera Mechanism." *ISAW Papers* 4
  (2012). http://dlib.nyu.edu/awdl/isaw/isaw-papers/4/ (open access).

**Recommended launch.** Gear-train schematic + caption — high visual interest,
completely original artwork, strongest story in the group.

### 3. Roman concrete — stronger after 2,000 years

**The hook.** Ancient Roman harbour structures (e.g. Caesarea Maritima, Baiae)
are still standing after 2,000 years of seawater immersion, while modern
Portland-cement concrete in marine environments fails within decades. The reason:
Roman *opus caementicium* used volcanic ash (pozzolana) mixed with seawater and
lime. A 2017 study (Jackson et al.) found that the alkaline seawater reaction
produces tobermorite crystals that strengthen over time rather than weakening —
the exact opposite of modern concrete. A 2023 follow-up (Masic et al., *Science*)
identified self-healing cracks via lime clast dissolution.

**Visual direction.**
- Option A: The Roman concrete recipe as a mock ingredient label — *pozzolana* /
  *aqua marina* / *calx* / volcanic tuff — with measured proportions adapted from
  Vitruvius Book II, ch. 6.
- Option B: Diagram of the tobermorite crystal growth mechanism (original schematic
  from the paper descriptions; the published figures are under journal copyright —
  redraw from the text description only).
- Option C: Comparison timeline — "Portland cement: fails 50 yrs / Roman
  pozzolana concrete: 2,000 yrs and growing" — pure text, no PD dependency.

**License.** The Vitruvius text (*De Architectura*, c. 30 BC) is fully PD. The 2023
*Science* paper figures are copyrighted — DO NOT use; redraw or use text only.
Original typographic layout requires no license.

**Sources.**
- Vitruvius, *De Architectura* Book II, ch. 6 (c. 30 BC, PD). Translation: Morris
  H. Morgan, 1914 (also PD). Online: Project Gutenberg.
- Jackson, M.D. et al. "Unlocking the secrets of Al-tobermorite in Roman seawater
  concrete." *American Mineralogist* 102 (2017), pp. 1435–1450.
  DOI 10.2138/am-2017-5993.
- Masic, A. et al. "Riddle of Roman concrete solved." *Science* 382 (2023), pp.
  1088–1093. DOI 10.1126/science.adg9309. (Cited result is citable fact; figure
  is copyrighted — redraw only.)

**Recommended launch.** Option A mock ingredient label — immediately legible,
requires no illustration, the Vitruvius proportions are historically cited.

### 4. Voynich manuscript — most debated book in the world

**The hook.** The Voynich manuscript is a 240-page illustrated codex, probably
written in early 15th-century northern Italy. Its text has never been deciphered
despite a century of cryptanalysis. Its illustrations show unidentified plants,
astronomical diagrams, bathing women, and cosmological charts. Statistical analysis
confirms the text obeys Zipf's law (consistent with a natural language) but no
known language or cipher matches it. The manuscript was donated to Yale's Beinecke
Rare Book Library in 1969 and is catalogued as MS 408.

**Visual direction.**
- Yale Beinecke Library has digitised the entire manuscript and released scans under
  CC BY-NC 3.0. The NC clause prevents commercial use of those scans directly.
  **Do not use Yale scans for commercial products without a separate license.**
- Safe alternative: derive an original line-art interpretation of one of the
  distinctive astronomical circular diagrams (f69r, f69v — the zodiac/calendar pages).
  The underlying manuscript content (15th century) is PD; only the Yale photographic
  reproduction is under CC BY-NC. A hand-drawn or computationally derived
  interpretation of the diagram structure is original artwork.
- Caption: "MS 408, c. 1404–1438 — still undeciphered."

**License.** Manuscript content: PD (pre-1527). Yale Beinecke scans: CC BY-NC 3.0 —
NOT usable for commercial shirts. **Use only original artwork derived from the
manuscript's structure.** Document this license decision in any ADR for this design.

**Sources.**
- Yale Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library, MS 408. Digitised at:
  https://beinecke.library.yale.edu/collections/highlights/voynich-manuscript
  (accessed 2026-06-25). License: CC BY-NC 3.0.
- Rugg, G. "The mystery of the Voynich manuscript." *Scientific American* 291:1
  (2004), pp. 104–109.
- Timm, T. & Schinner, A. "Voynich Text Analysis." *PLOS ONE* 2019.
  DOI 10.1371/journal.pone.0214786. (Open access.)

**Recommended launch.** Proceed only with an original line-art interpretation;
explicitly flag the Yale NC clause in the brief before production.

### 5. Damascus steel — carbon nanotubes in a medieval sword

**The hook.** Damascus (wootz) steel, produced in India and the Middle East from
approximately the 3rd century BC to the 18th century AD, was legendary for its
superior edge retention and the distinctive wavy *damask* pattern on the blade
surface. In 2006, Reibold et al. (*Nature*) used electron microscopy to show that
authentic Damascus blades contain multi-walled carbon nanotubes and cementite
nanowires embedded in the matrix — formed unintentionally by the ancient smelting
process. The production technique was lost around 1750 AD, probably due to
depletion of specific ore sources that provided the trace elements (vanadium,
chromium) needed to produce the nanotube-forming carbide chemistry.

**Visual direction.**
- Option A: The wavy damask pattern as a shirt graphic, with caption
  "Damascus steel, c. 300 BC – AD 1750: carbon nanotubes, accidentally."
- Option B: Schematic of the multi-wall nanotube / cementite nanowire structure —
  a clean cross-section diagram (original, derived from the paper description).
- Option C: Typographic — the chemical formula for cementite (Fe₃C) and the
  nanotube allotrope (C) side by side with the dates.

**License.** The historical blade pattern is PD by age. The Reibold et al. 2006
*Nature* paper figures are copyrighted — redraw from text description only.
Original diagram requires no license.

**Sources.**
- Reibold, M. et al. "Carbon nanotubes in an ancient Damascus sabre." *Nature* 444
  (2006), p. 286. DOI 10.1038/444286a. (Brief communication; cited result is a
  citable fact; journal figure is copyrighted — redraw only.)
- Verhoeven, J.D. et al. "The Mystery of Damascus Blades." *Scientific American*
  284:1 (2001), pp. 74–79.

**Recommended launch.** Option A typographic + wavy pattern — zero illustration
complexity, visually recognisable, story lands in the caption.
