# Philosophy Theme Group

**Scope:** Products built around philosophy's most memorable thought experiments,
arguments, and moments of clarity — the ideas that are genuinely difficult to shake
once you've heard them, and that non-philosophers recognise and enjoy arguing about.

**Why it fits the shop:** Philosophy's best outputs are brief, precise, and often
funny in hindsight. "I think, therefore I am" is a complete argument in five words.
The trolley problem has no answer but everyone has an opinion. These ideas demand
no prior knowledge, travel well, and provoke a response — exactly what the shop needs.
The owner's physics background makes the more rigorous analytical sub-line (Zeno,
Gödel-adjacent philosophy, logic paradoxes) natural territory.

## Sub-ideas

### 1. Cogito ergo sum — Descartes
Descartes, *Discours de la méthode* (1637, Leiden) and *Principia Philosophiae* (1644):
"cogito, ergo sum" — the one thing you cannot doubt is that you are doubting.

- **Product options:**
  - **Typography-only shirt:** "cogito, ergo sum." in period-appropriate roman type
    (Garamond), with "Descartes, 1637" as a small attribution line
  - **Diagram variant:** the evil demon argument as a logic tree
    (is anything certain? → only that I think → I exist); clean on dark fabric
  - **Funny translation:** "I overthink, therefore I am" (original copy, avoids
    translation copyright issues; the Latin is PD)
- **Slogan options:**
  - "cogito, ergo sum. — Descartes, 1637."
  - "I think. Therefore I am. (Descartes hasn't been disproved yet.)"
  - "The one thing Descartes was certain of."
- **Visual:** typography-only is lowest cost and highest potential; the original French
  text is PD (no translation needed — the Latin tag is universally known)
- **Source:** Descartes, R. (1637). *Discours de la méthode*. Leiden: Jan Maire.
  [Project Gutenberg: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/13846 — PD]

### 2. Plato's cave allegory
Plato, *Republic* Book VII (c. 375 BC): prisoners in a cave see only shadows of real
objects on the wall behind them and mistake the shadows for reality.

- **Diagram shirt/poster:** cave cross-section — fire, object, shadow, chained figures
  — drawn in clean line art; very visualisable in ~50 lines of Matplotlib or Inkscape
- **Slogans:**
  - "You are looking at the shadows." (direct, applicable to any context)
  - "The fire is behind you. — Plato, c. 375 BC"
  - "They thought the shadows were real. — Plato, Republic VII"
- **Visual direction:** side-view diagram of the cave in clean vector style (not the
  famous Flickr-era diagram which has a copyright owner — create original); prisoners
  at bottom, fire at right, object between fire and wall, shadows projected left
- **License:** PD (pre-Socratic / classical antiquity)
- **Note:** Cave diagram is a standard geometry exercise — creating original line art
  avoids any derivative-work issues

### 3. Zeno's paradoxes
Zeno of Elea (c. 490–430 BC): Achilles gives a tortoise a head start and can never
catch it, because each time he reaches where the tortoise was, it has moved further.
Resolved by the convergence of geometric series (Σ(1/2)ⁿ = 1), but the argument is
still philosophically contested.

- **Diagram shirt:** Achilles and the tortoise positions at t=0, t=1/2, t=3/4, t=7/8…
  with the geometric series converging visually; copy: "Achilles never catches the
  tortoise. — Zeno, c. 450 BC. (He does. Eventually.)"
- **Slogans:**
  - "Achilles never catches the tortoise. (He does.)"
  - "Σ(1/2)ⁿ = 1, but Zeno wasn't convinced."
  - "Resolved in 1821. Still argued about."  (Cauchy convergence criterion, 1821)
- **Visual direction:** horizontal timeline with Achilles (A) and tortoise (T) at
  each stage; gap halving each time; Matplotlib ~30 lines; or pure typography
- **License:** PD (c. 450 BC); Cauchy 1821 well within PD threshold
- **Cross-reference:** connects to logic-and-computation (infinite processes, halting)

### 4. The trolley problem
Philippa Foot (1967). A runaway trolley will kill five people; you can pull a lever to
redirect it onto a side track where it will kill one person. Do you pull the lever?
Judith Jarvis Thomson (1985) added the "footbridge variant" (push a large man off a
bridge to stop the trolley).

- **Product options:**
  - **Diagram shirt:** top-down track schematic with 5 figures vs. 1 figure and a lever
  - **Two-shirt set:** "Pull the lever" / "Don't pull the lever" — both available, buyers
    choose; forces friends to debate
  - **Stats variant:** "73% of respondents pull the lever. 27% do not. Everyone is right."
    (citing real philosophy survey: Awad et al. 2018, *Nature*)
- **Slogans:**
  - "Pull the lever? — Foot, 1967."
  - "5 or 1. The answer is obvious. Except when it isn't."
  - "Moral philosophy, reduced to a diagram."
- **Visual direction:** clean top-down SVG/Matplotlib schematic; dark shirt; tracks in
  white; figures as simple silhouettes; lever position annotated
- **License:** The trolley problem is an ethical thought experiment — the concept is not
  copyrightable; any original track diagram is original work. Foot (1967) is in
  copyright, but citing her name and year is attribution, not reproduction.
- **Source:** Foot, P. (1967). "The Problem of Abortion and the Doctrine of Double
  Effect." *Oxford Review*, 5, 5–15. [concept PD; cite for attribution]
  Awad, E., et al. (2018). "The Moral Machine Experiment." *Nature*, 563, 59–64.
  [for the 73% statistic]

### 5. Occam's Razor
William of Ockham (c. 1287–1347): *Entia non sunt multiplicanda praeter necessitatem* —
"entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity." The simplest explanation consistent
with the evidence is preferred.

- **Typography-only shirt:** the original Latin maxim in period type; or the common
  English formulation: "The simplest explanation is usually correct."
- **Slogans:**
  - "Entia non sunt multiplicanda praeter necessitatem. — Ockham, c. 1320."
  - "When you hear hoofbeats, think horses. — William of Ockham."
  - "The razor. — Ockham, c. 1320."
- **Funny variant:** the razor applied to modern life — "You probably just forgot to
  charge it. — Occam's Razor" (original copy; no reproduction of source text)
- **License:** PD (medieval; well before any copyright threshold)
- **Note:** Occam's Razor is widely parodied but rarely attributed precisely — the Latin
  phrase is a PD philosophical statement and an original clean typographic treatment
  differentiates from the sea of generic "razor" merchandise

### 6. Wittgenstein's language games (stretch goal)
Wittgenstein, *Philosophical Investigations* (1953). Meaning is use — language is a set
of games, each with its own rules. "The limits of my language mean the limits of my world."

- **Slogan:** "Die Grenzen meiner Sprache bedeuten die Grenzen meiner Welt. — Wittgenstein, 1921."
  (*Tractatus*, §5.6 — 1921, PD; original German)
- **Note:** this is a stretch-goal — the audience is more niche than the trolley problem
  or cogito; flagged here for completeness if a "logic/language" sub-line develops

## Visualisation and automation notes

- **Plato's cave diagram:** ~60 lines of Matplotlib; fire at right (orange dot),
  straight-line projections to wall (left), prisoners as dots; minimal but legible
  ```python
  # sketch:
  import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
  # Draw cave outline, fire, prisoners, shadows — all original geometry
  ```
- **Zeno timeline:** Matplotlib `annotate` + `hlines`; Achilles and tortoise positions
  at each step; geometric series visible in spacing
- **Trolley problem:** Matplotlib patches (tracks as lines, figures as simple glyphs);
  ~40 lines; no external assets needed
- **Typography designs (Descartes, Occam):** EB Garamond (SIL OFL 1.1) or Cormorant
  Garamond — period-appropriate Roman typefaces, free and commercially licensed
