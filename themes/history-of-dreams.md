# Theme group: History of dreams

A shirt series about humanity's recurring ambitions — things people have dreamed of,
reached for, and sometimes discovered were impossible, or possible but not in the way
imagined. Each sub-idea has a twist: the dream and the reality diverge in a
surprising, wryly funny way. Tone is dry and knowing, not mocking.

**Shop fit:** HIGH — rich in PD material (18th–19th century engravings, scientific
papers); the "dream vs. reality" narrative structure is inherently memorable. Most
sub-ideas require only typography + one original or PD illustration.

---

## Sub-idea 1 — The Mechanical Turk: first AI (sort of)

**Concept:** In 1770, Wolfgang von Kempelen unveiled a chess-playing "automaton" —
a turbaned wooden figure seated at a cabinet full of machinery. It beat nearly
everyone who played it, including Napoleon and Benjamin Franklin. After von Kempelen's
death it toured Europe for decades. The secret: the cabinet hid a human chess master
crouching among the gears. The "AI" was a person all along.

**Visual direction:**

Option A — *Original engraving style (PD source):*  
The 1784 Windisch engraving from *Über den Schachspieler des Herrn von Kempelen*
(Joseph Friedrich zu Racknitz's 1789 Leipzig pamphlet has a detailed cross-section
diagram). Both are PD (well over 200 years old, originally published in the
Holy Roman Empire). The cross-section showing the hidden human is the most striking image.

Option B — *Typography-only:*  
```
THE MECHANICAL TURK
Chess-playing automaton, Vienna, 1770.
Mechanism: human.
```

**Copy options:**
- *"1770: the world's first AI. Hidden inside: a human chess grandmaster."*
- *"Von Kempelen's Turk, 1770. Turing test passed. Method: fraud."*
- *"The first machine intelligence. Powered by: a person."*

**License:**  
- Windisch, K.G., *Über den Schachspieler des Herrn von Kempelen* (Basle, 1783). PD.
- Racknitz, J.F., *Über den Schachspieler des Herrn von Kempelen* (Leipzig, 1789). PD.
- Cross-section engraving from Racknitz 1789 is on Wikimedia Commons (PD-old-100).

**Source:**

**Production priority:** HIGH — the "AI powered by a human" punchline is timely and

## Sub-idea 2 — Victorian electrical séances: the telegraph to the dead

**Concept:** In March 1848 the Fox sisters of Hydesville, New York, reported
mysterious rappings they claimed were communications from the dead. Within months,
séances were fashionable across the US and UK. At exactly the same time, Samuel
Morse's telegraph was spreading across the continent — the first technology to let
people communicate instantaneously at distance. Newspapers called it "the spiritual
telegraph." Edison told a reporter in 1920 that he was working on a device to contact
the dead (though no such device was ever produced).

- *"Edison, 1920: 'I am working on a device to contact the dead.' Patent: none."*

## Sub-idea 3 — Theory of everything: the perpetually revised draft

**Concept:** Every generation of physicists has believed they were close to a final
unified theory, and every generation has been wrong — but each "wrong" version turned
out to be profoundly correct within its domain. Newton's mechanics (1687) unified
terrestrial and celestial physics. Maxwell's equations (1865) unified electricity,
magnetism, and light. Einstein's relativity (1915) unified space, time, and gravity.
The Standard Model (1970s) unified three fundamental forces. String theory (1980s–)
remains unconfirmed. The shirt captures this as a software version history.

**Visual direction — software patch-note format:**

```
THEORY OF EVERYTHING
v1.0 — Newton (1687):  F = ma, gravitation. ✓ STABLE
v2.0 — Maxwell (1865): ∇·E = ρ/ε₀, light. ✓ STABLE
v3.0 — Einstein (1915): Gμν = 8πGTμν. ✓ STABLE
v4.0 — Standard Model (1970s): SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1). ✓ STABLE
v5.0 — String / M-theory (1980s–): 10⁴⁹⁹ vacua. ⚠ BETA
v6.0 — ?
```

**Copy options:**
- *"Draft in progress since 1687."*
- *"v5.0: still in beta."*
- *"Every physicist's dream. Every version: a revision."*

**License:**  
- All equations (Newton, Maxwell, Einstein, Standard Model gauge group) are
  mathematical and scientific facts — not copyrightable.
- Software-patchnote aesthetic is an original composition referencing a widely
  understood format; no trademark or copyright concern.

**Source:**  
- Newton, *Principia Mathematica* (1687). PD.
- Maxwell, "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field," *Phil. Trans. R. Soc.*
  155 (1865). PD.
- Einstein, "Die Feldgleichungen der Gravitation," *Sitzungsberichte der
  Preussischen Akademie der Wissenschaften* (1915). PD.
- Glashow/Salam/Weinberg Standard Model (1967–1973) — facts and formulae PD.
- Accessed: 2026-06-25.

**Production priority:** HIGH — pure typography, zero illustration cost; equations
are verifiable by the physics-owner; the patch-note format is an immediately legible
nerd hook.

## Sub-idea 4 — Perpetual motion machines: pending since 1230

**Concept:** Humanity has dreamed of a device that runs forever without energy input
since at least the 13th century. One of the earliest documented designs is the
overbalanced wheel sketched by French architect Villard de Honnecourt around 1230 in
his portfolio (BnF MS Fr 19093, folio 22v). The dream has persisted through Bessler's
wheel (1712, claimed to work; later found fraudulent), Redheffer's machine (1812,
exposed in Philadelphia), and continues today on YouTube. No perpetual motion machine
has ever been confirmed to work; the Second Law of Thermodynamics explains why.

**Visual direction:**

Option A — *PD illustration + mock patent:*  
The Villard de Honnecourt overbalanced wheel sketch (BnF Gallica — PD-old-100,
manuscript from c. 1230). Add mock patent label:  
```
PATENT PENDING
Application filed: c. 1230
Status: pending
Verdict: see Second Law of Thermodynamics (1850)
```

Option B — *Typography only:*  
```
PERPETUAL MOTION MACHINE
Working since:  never
Disproven:  1850 (Second Law of Thermodynamics)
Patent pending:  since 1230
```

**Copy options:**
- *"Patent pending since 1230."*
- *"Every generation builds one. None have worked. Second Law: still enforced."*
- *"Villard de Honnecourt, 1230. Status: still pending."*

**License:**  
- Villard de Honnecourt portfolio, BnF MS Fr 19093 (c. 1230). Available on Gallica
  (gallica.bnf.fr). Gallica's digitisation of works clearly in the public domain
  (pre-1927) is released as PD-old-100 on Wikimedia Commons — commercially usable.
- All historical facts and thermodynamic laws are not copyrightable.

**Source:**

  Gallica permalink: https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/btv1b10509412z. Accessed 2026-06-25.

**Production priority:** HIGH — mock patent format is zero illustration cost; Villard
sketch is PD and visually striking as a texture or background. Easiest to produce in
this group.

## Cross-theme connections

- **Mechanical Turk + Theory of Everything** both play with the gap between the
  apparent miracle and the actual mechanism — "AI powered by a human" vs. "the final
  theory that keeps needing revision."
- **Victorian séances + Perpetual motion** are both about chasing something that
  physics rules out — contact with the dead (no signal), work from nothing (no energy).
- The group pairs naturally with **Dead and defunct scientific theories** (separate
  theme file: `themes/dead-theories.md`).

## Recommended launch order

1. **Perpetual motion** — pure typography option, zero illustration cost, immediately
   funny.
2. **Theory of everything** — patch-note format, zero illustration cost, strong STEM
   hook; pairs well with the Maxwell's equations design.
3. **Mechanical Turk** — PD engraving available; "AI powered by a human" is timely.
4. **Victorian séances** — mock telegram is typography-only; Edison quote is the hook.
