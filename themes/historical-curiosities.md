# Theme group: Non-trivial historical curiosities

One-story-per-shirt concept. Each shirt is built around a single obscure historical
fact so surprising it makes the wearer want to explain it. Nerd-shop sweet spot: the
physics owner can verify every claim; the audience feels smarter after reading the back.

**Shop fit:** HIGH — sourced facts, no contested claims, original visuals possible
for every sub-idea. Strongest opener is Antikythera (visual richness + ancient
computer hook) followed by Roman concrete (ingredient-list format, zero illustration
cost).

---

## Sub-idea 1 — Silphium: consumed to extinction

**Concept:** The only plant known to have been eaten to extinction. Ancient Greeks and
Romans prized it so highly (currency, medicine, flavour, contraceptive) that they
harvested it from its only known habitat — the coastline around Cyrene (modern Libya)
— until none remained. The last recorded specimen was presented to Emperor Nero
(Pliny, *Natural History*, XIX.38–39, c. 77 AD).

**Visual direction:**
- Front: Cyrenean tetradrachm silhouette showing the silphium plant (the coin design)
  — one of the most reproduced Greek coin motifs.
- Copy option 1: *"Cyrene, c. 100 BC — the only plant mankind ate into extinction."*
- Copy option 2: mock product label: *"SILPHIUM — supply: exhausted. Est. 1st century
  AD. No known substitutes."* (original label artwork, no third-party asset)

**License:** The coin design is ~2,300 years old and entirely PD. Any line-art
reconstruction is original. No photography of the coin is required — outline only.

**Source:**  
- Pliny the Elder, *Naturalis Historia*, Book XIX, §§ 38–39 (c. 77 AD). PD.
- John Scarborough, "Theophrastus on Herbals and Herbal Remedies," *Journal of the
  History of Biology*, Vol. 11, No. 2 (Autumn 1978), pp. 353–385. [paywall, facts
  are generally accepted]
- Coin image reference: British Museum online collection (PD photograph policy for
  coins pre-1300 AD).
- Accessed: 2026-06-25.

**Production priority:** Medium — design is strong but coin silhouette needs careful
redrawing; label variant is lower effort.

## Sub-idea 2 — Antikythera mechanism: ancient analogue computer

**Concept:** In 1901 a Greek sponge diver found a corroded bronze lump in a Roman-era
shipwreck off Antikythera. It turned out to be a 37-gear mechanical computer from
~100 BC that tracked the Metonic cycle, Saros cycle, and predicted eclipses. Derek de
Solla Price reconstructed the gear train in 1974 (*Gears from the Greeks*, American
Philosophical Society).

**Visual direction:**
- A clean schematic of the gear train (derived from de Solla Price 1974 / Freeth
  et al. 2021 *Scientific Reports* — gear connectivity diagram, not the physical
  fragment photograph).
- Copy: *"Antikythera, c. 100 BC — 37 gears, bronze, analogue. The first computer."*
- Alternative: gear-train outline only, no copy, caption below.

**License:**  
- De Solla Price 1974 publication is PD (over 50 years old, US scientific
  publication).
- Freeth et al. 2021 gear-connectivity diagram published in *Scientific Reports*
  under CC BY 4.0 — commercially usable with attribution.
- **Do NOT use photographs of the physical Antikythera fragments** — those are held
  by the National Archaeological Museum Athens; their reproduction terms are
  restrictive. Diagram only.

**Source:**  
- Derek J. de Solla Price, *Gears from the Greeks*, Transactions of the American
  Philosophical Society, Vol. 64, No. 7 (1974). PD.
- Tony Freeth et al., "A Model of the Cosmos in the ancient Greek Antikythera
  Mechanism," *Scientific Reports* 11, 5821 (2021). CC BY 4.0.
  https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-84310-w. Accessed 2026-06-25.

**Production priority:** HIGH — visually rich, famous story, STEM audience hook.
Recommended as launch product for this theme group.

## Sub-idea 3 — Roman concrete: stronger than modern concrete

**Concept:** Roman concrete (*opus caementicium*) mixed volcanic pozzolan, seawater,
and lime. Two thousand years later it is still intact — while modern Portland cement
cracks within decades. The reason: seawater percolating through the concrete slowly
grows tobermorite crystals in the pozzolan voids, creating a self-healing matrix.
Masic et al. 2023 confirmed the "hot lime" mixing hypothesis.

**Visual direction (two options):**

Option A — *Mock ingredient label:*  
```
ROMAN CONCRETE
Ingredients: pozzolana (volcanic), seawater (Tyrrhenian),
  quicklime (calcined limestone), aggregate (tufa, pottery shards)
Serving size: one harbour wall
Shelf life: ≥ 2,000 years
Modern equivalent: none
```
No illustration needed; pure typography on dark or light background.

Option B — *Schematic:* cross-section showing pozzolan + lime → tobermorite crystal
growth (simplified diagram; original artwork).

**License:**  
- Vitruvius, *De Architectura*, Book II, Chapter 6 (~25 BC). Well PD. Text quotes are
  fine.
- Masic et al. 2023 paper published in *Science Advances* under CC BY-NC; the
  underlying facts (the tobermorite mechanism, ingredient ratios) are not
  copyrightable.
- Original label artwork and diagram = no license concern.

**Source:**  
- Admir Masic et al., "Seawater-Activated Self-Healing in Roman Maritime Concrete,"
  *Science Advances*, Vol. 9, No. 1 (2023). DOI: 10.1126/sciadv.add1602.
  Accessed 2026-06-25.
- Vitruvius, *De Architectura*, Book II, §6 (c. 25 BC). PD.

**Production priority:** HIGH — ingredient label format is zero-illustration-cost,
very easy to produce. Strong "wait, what?" reaction.

## Sub-idea 4 — Voynich manuscript: still unread

**Concept:** A 15th-century illustrated manuscript (carbon-dated 1404–1438) written
in an undeciphered script, filled with botanical, astronomical, and biological
illustrations that match nothing in known catalogues. Despite decades of analysis by
cryptographers and linguists (including WWII codebreakers), no one has decoded it.

**Visual direction:**
- A single striking folio page as background (blurred or stylised, original
  interpretation) with copy: *"Written c. 1420. Still unread."*
- Or: an original ink-style line-art recreation of one of the plant illustrations
  (the "blue flower" or "onion root" folios) — completely original artwork inspired
  by the style.

**License — CRITICAL WARNING:**  
- The Yale Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library scans of the Voynich manuscript
  are available online under **CC BY-NC 3.0** — the NC clause means they are
  **NOT commercially usable on merchandise** without a separate licence agreement.
- **Do NOT use the Yale scans directly** on a product.
- The underlying manuscript is PD (pre-1927); only the Yale photographic scan
  reproduction is under CC BY-NC.
- **Safe path:** commission or generate original line-art inspired by the style —
  this is an original work and carries no third-party licence burden.

**Source:**  
- Yale Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library, MS 408. CC BY-NC 3.0.
  https://beinecke.library.yale.edu/collections/highlights/voynich-manuscript.
  Accessed 2026-06-25.

**Production priority:** LOW (until original artwork is produced) — strong concept,
but blocked on illustration work. Do not use Yale scans. Do last in this group.

## Sub-idea 5 — Damascus steel: medieval nanotech

**Concept:** Damascus steel (wootz) blades showed a wavy surface pattern (damask)
prized for sharpness and beauty. In 2006, Reibold et al. published in *Nature* that
transmission electron microscopy revealed the blades contained multi-wall carbon
nanotubes — a nanostructure that would not be independently discovered for centuries.
Medieval swordsmiths, by trial and error, produced nanotube-reinforced steel.

**Visual direction:**
- Front: stylised cross-section diagram of the wavy damask pattern at two scales —
  macro (wavy blade pattern) and micro (nanotube schematic).
- Copy: *"Damascus, c. 900 AD — multi-wall carbon nanotubes. Rediscovered: 1991."*
- Alternative: wavy damask pattern texture only (no diagram), copy on the back.

**License:**  
- Reibold et al. 2006 *Nature* paper reports the facts; the facts are not
  copyrightable. The TEM micrograph images from the paper are © Springer Nature;
  do not reproduce them. Use original schematic artwork only.

**Source:**  
- M. Reibold et al., "Carbon nanotubes in an ancient Damascus sabre,"
  *Nature* 444, 286 (2006). https://doi.org/10.1038/444286a. Accessed 2026-06-25.

**Production priority:** Medium — wavy damask pattern is visually distinctive; the
nanotube fact is a perfect STEM hook. Requires original diagram (no photograph).

## Cross-theme notes

- **Silphium + Voynich** can form a "lost and unrecoverable" mini-series.
- **Antikythera + Damascus steel** form an "ancient tech that sounds impossible" pair.
- **Roman concrete** stands alone well; also fits a "things Romans did better" series.

## Recommended launch order

1. **Antikythera** — gear-train diagram is a visually rich anchor; CC BY 4.0 source available.
2. **Roman concrete** — zero illustration cost; label format is immediately legible.
3. **Damascus steel** — requires original diagram; strong hook for materials-science audience.
4. **Silphium** — coin silhouette needs careful redraw; label variant is easier.
5. **Voynich** — requires original illustration work; do last.
