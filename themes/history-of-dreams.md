# Theme: History of Dreams

> Shop fit: shirts about what humanity tried to build before it knew better —
> and occasionally succeeded. The humour is gentle; the history is real.

---

## Scope and rationale

"Dreams" here means collective human ambitions that seemed impossible and either
came true (flight, transmutation analogue), were eventually debunked (perpetual
motion, immortality elixir), or remain productively strange (talking to the dead
via electricity). Each shirt pairs the dream with the moment it crystallised or
collapsed. All use PD source material or original artwork.

---

## Sub-ideas

### 1. Dreams of robots, immortality, and fulfilled ambitions

**The hook.** Three layered stories that share a structure: ridiculous dream →
serious attempt → surprising outcome.

**a. The Mechanical Turk (dreams of robots)**
The Mechanical Turk, unveiled by Wolfgang von Kempelen in 1770, appeared to be a
chess-playing automaton — a turbaned figure seated at a cabinet with an exposed
clockwork mechanism. It defeated Benjamin Franklin, Frederick the Great, and
Napoleon. The secret: a skilled human chess player was concealed inside the
cabinet, operating the arm via a system of magnets. The hoax ran for 84 years
across three continents. It is, however, the direct conceptual ancestor of Amazon
Mechanical Turk (2005), named after it. The dream of an autonomous thinking
machine outlasted the hoax.

- Visual: the original 1769 engraving from Karl Gottlieb von Windisch's
  *Inanimate Reason* (1784, PD). Fine linework chess automaton with the cabinet open.
- Source: Windisch, K.G. von. *Inanimate Reason* (1784, PD; digitised at Internet
  Archive). Engraving is PD by age.

**b. Philosopher's stone (dreams of immortality)**
Alchemists pursued the *lapis philosophorum* — a substance that would transmute
base metals into gold and confer eternal life — for at least 1,500 years. The
pursuit was not merely mystical; it drove real chemistry. Robert Boyle, Isaac
Newton, and Gottfried Leibniz all investigated alchemy. Newton spent more time
writing about alchemy than about physics. The dream produced systematic laboratory
method, distillation apparatus, and the periodic classification of substances —
then died when Lavoisier demonstrated conservation of mass in 1789.

- Visual: Robert Fludd's 1617 *Utriusque Cosmi* elemental diagram (already in
  `themes/alchemy.md`), or a shirt reading:
  "Isaac Newton's other project: 1,000,000 words on alchemy / *Principia*: 500 pages."
- Source: Newton's alchemical manuscripts at Cambridge (Chymistry of Isaac Newton
  project, Indiana University, open access): https://webapp1.dlib.indiana.edu/newton/

**c. Flight (dream fulfilled)**
The Wright brothers flew at Kitty Hawk on 17 December 1903 — 12 seconds, 120 feet.
Four years later, the US Army Signal Corps offered to buy the design for $25,000.
The dream took approximately 2,000 years from Archytas of Tarentum's reported
steam-pigeon (c. 350 BC) to practical realisation.

- This sub-idea overlaps with the Patent Drawings theme (see `themes/patent-drawings.md`).
  Best used as part of a "dream → reality" series shirt showing timeline milestones,
  not as a standalone concept.

**Recommended shirt.** The Mechanical Turk engraving + caption
"First artificial intelligence, 1770. (The secret was inside.)" — high visual
impact, clean PD source, instantly readable.

### 2. Dreams of talking to the dead — Victorian electrical séances

**The hook.** When the electric telegraph was demonstrated in the 1840s–1850s,
mainstream newspapers and scientists genuinely theorised that electricity might
provide a channel to communicate with the dead. The telegraph seemed to transmit
thought instantaneously across invisible distances — exactly what a séance claimed
to do. The Fox sisters' 1848 "spirit rappings" in Hydesville, New York, launched
modern Spiritualism in the same decade as Morse's 1844 "What hath God wrought"
transmission. The similarity was not lost on contemporaries: Scientific American
ran articles in 1854 debating whether electromagnetism explained spirit contact.
Thomas Edison claimed in 1920 to be working on a "spirit telephone."

**Visual direction.**
- Option A: A mock telegram form (19th-century Western Union format, PD) addressed
  "TO: [deceased person] / MESSAGE: Are you there? / TRANSMISSION: Pending."
- Option B: A Victorian telegraph key with a caption "Long-distance séance, 1848."
- Option C: Edison's 1920 newspaper quote about his spirit telephone, typeset as
  a newspaper clipping (original typographic design, no reproduction of the
  actual newspaper needed).

**License.** Western Union telegram formats from pre-1927 publications are PD.
19th-century newspaper engravings of telegraph equipment are PD by age. All
typographic interpretations are original.

**Sources.**
- Scientific American, "Electricity and the Spirits" (exact title varies; multiple
  issues 1852–1855 discussed the parallel; the archive is PD by age).
- Sconce, J. *Haunted Media: Electronic Presence from Telegraphy to Television*
  (Duke University Press, 2000). Non-PD but cites primary sources.
- "Thomas Edison's spirit phone," *Scientific American* December 1920 (PD archive).

**Recommended launch.** Option A mock telegram — immediately legible, no complex
illustration required, clever concept.

### 3. Dreams of understanding everything — the quest for a theory of everything

**The hook.** Every generation has believed it was close to a complete theory of
the universe. Newton's *Principia* (1687) seemed to reduce all motion to three
laws. Maxwell's equations (1865) unified electricity, magnetism, and light.
Einstein's general relativity (1915) resolved Newton's gravity. The Standard
Model (1970s) catalogued all known particles. String theory (1980s–present)
claims to unify everything — and is still incomplete. Each breakthrough revealed
a deeper layer. The shirt is a visual history of successive "final" theories.

**Visual direction.**
- Option A: A stacked timeline — Newton 1687 / Maxwell 1865 / Einstein 1915 /
  Dirac 1928 / Standard Model 1970s / String theory ? — each labelled as
  "Theory of Everything" with a footnote saying "revised".
- Option B: A single equation "Theory of Everything: ______" with a blank, evoking
  the incompleteness of the project.
- Option C: Each physicist's most famous equation in a vertical list with their
  name and year — reading as a "theory of everything" draft that is perpetually
  in progress.

**License.** All equations are mathematical facts — not copyrightable. All
historical discoveries are PD facts. Typographic layout is original.

**Sources.**
- Dirac, P.A.M. "The quantum theory of the electron." *Proc. R. Soc. London* A 117
  (1928), pp. 610–624. (PD in most jurisdictions by age.)
- Weinberg, S. *Dreams of a Final Theory* (1992). Non-PD — use only as background
  reference; do not reproduce text.
- The Standard Model Lagrangian is widely published; original typeset version is
  created by the designer. No copyright claim applies to mathematical expressions.

**Recommended launch.** Option A timeline — the repeating "revised" joke lands
visually without needing dense text.

### 4. Dreams of unlimited energy — perpetual motion machines

**The hook.** Perpetual motion machines — devices that produce more energy than
they consume — have been proposed, patented, and occasionally demonstrated to paying
audiences for at least 700 years. The most famous examples:

- **Villard de Honnecourt's overbalanced wheel** (c. 1230) — earliest known
  diagram; a wheel with hinged mallets designed to always have more weight on the
  descending side. Never worked.
- **Bessler's wheel** (Johann Ernst Elias Bessler, 1712–1727) — spun continuously
  for 54 days under observation in Kassel; examined by Leibniz, Peter the Great,
  and other luminaries. Secret never revealed; Bessler died having never collected
  the prize. The mechanism remains unknown but almost certainly involved a concealed
  operator.
- **The free-energy cottage industry** — persists to the present day; the US Patent
  Office has refused to examine perpetual motion applications without a working model
  since 1911.

**Visual direction.**
- Option A: Villard de Honnecourt's original 1230 wheel diagram — from *Livre de
  portraiture* (c. 1230, MS Fr 19093, Bibliothèque Nationale de France). The BnF
  Gallica scans of medieval manuscripts are generally PD-old-100; verify the specific
  folio. The diagram shows an overbalanced wheel with hammer-heads — visually
  arresting.
- Option B: A mock patent label — "Patent Pending since 1230 / Still pending." —
  referencing the USPTO 1911 policy.
- Option C: A timeline of all perpetual motion machines with caption "All denied —
  the energy went somewhere."

**License.** The Villard de Honnecourt manuscript folios are PD-old-100. The BnF
Gallica platform publishes them; confirm per-folio status before production. Bessler
wheel diagrams from 18th-century prints are PD by age. All typographic designs are
original.

**Sources.**
- Villard de Honnecourt, *Livre de portraiture* (c. 1230). MS Fr 19093, BnF Gallica.
  URL: https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/btv1b10509412z (accessed 2026-06-25).
  License: PD-old-100 (confirm per folio).
- Ord-Hume, A.W.J.G. *Perpetual Motion: The History of an Obsession* (1977). Non-PD;
  use as background reference only.
- Simanek, D.E. "Perpetual Motion: Illusion and Reality." Lock Haven University
  (web resource, open access). https://www.lhup.edu/~dsimanek/museum/unwork.htm

**Recommended launch.** Option A Villard wheel diagram — oldest known perpetual
motion diagram, visually distinctive, completely PD, and the irony is self-evident.
