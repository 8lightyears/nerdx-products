# Theme Group: Dead and Defunct Scientific Theories

**Scope:** Theories that were coherent, taken seriously by working scientists, and
beautifully wrong. Each shirt is a memorial to a dead idea — paired, where possible,
with the experiment or observation that killed it.

**Why it fits the shop:** The "dead theory" format is a natural fit for people with a
science background. The humour is understated: the idea is stated earnestly on the shirt,
and either the date gives away that it was eventually refuted, or a brief "(It doesn't.)"
comment does the work. Visually, many of these theories produced striking apparatus
diagrams, interferometer schematics, and celestial maps — all PD, all beautiful.

**Shop angle:** "Memorial series — for ideas that made perfect sense at the time."

## Sub-idea 1: Luminiferous aether

**The theory:** Light is a transverse wave and must have a medium to propagate through,
just as sound needs air. The aether was that medium — permeating all space, at rest with
respect to absolute space, and detectable by the motion of the Earth through it.

**The experiment that killed it:** The Michelson-Morley experiment (1887). Albert A.
Michelson and Edward W. Morley, at what is now Case Western Reserve University, built a
stone interferometer on a mercury float to isolate it from vibration. They split a beam of
light, sent it along two perpendicular arms, and looked for a fringe shift as the Earth
moved through the aether. Result: null. No fringe shift. The aether did not affect the
speed of light along any direction.

The result puzzled physicists for 18 years. It contributed directly to Lorentz's length-
contraction hypothesis (1892) and, ultimately, to Einstein's special relativity (1905),
which discarded the aether entirely.

**Source:** Michelson, A.A. and Morley, E.W. "On the Relative Motion of the Earth and of
the Luminiferous Ether." *American Journal of Science* 34 (1887), pp. 333–345.
https://archive.org/details/sim_american-journal-of-science_1887-11_34_203 (accessed 2026-06-25).
PD (published 1887, no surviving copyright).

**PD diagram:** The paper contains two diagrams — the plan view of the interferometer
(Fig. 1) and the fringe pattern (Fig. 2). The interferometer plan view is iconic and
reproduced in every physics textbook. The diagram is in the 1887 paper, unambiguously PD.

**Visual direction:** Clean line redraw of the interferometer plan view + "Result: null, 1887."
Or use the original paper diagram directly (scan cleanup with ImageMagick). Minimal and
elegant.

**Shirt copy options:**
- "Luminiferous aether. — Null result, Michelson-Morley, 1887."
- "The aether was detected. / (It was not.)"
- Interferometer diagram only + "δ = 0.00"

## Sub-idea 2: Phlogiston theory

**The theory:** All combustible materials contain *phlogiston* — a fire-like substance
released during combustion. Metals "lose" phlogiston when they burn in air, forming a
calx (what we now call an oxide). Reducing the calx with charcoal (rich in phlogiston)
restored the metal. Air "absorbed" phlogiston from flames; closed-vessel flames went out
when air became "phlogisticated."

Proposed by Johann Becher (*Physica Subterranea*, 1667) and systematised by Georg Ernst
Stahl (*Zymotechnica Fundamentalis*, 1697; *Theoria Medica Vera*, 1708). The theory
elegantly unified combustion, smelting, and respiration. It was widely accepted for ~80
years.

**What killed it:** Antoine Lavoisier showed in a series of experiments (1772–1789) that
combustion gains, not loses, mass — the opposite of phlogiston theory's prediction. He
identified oxygen as the active component of air in combustion. Published: *Traité
Élémentaire de Chimie* (Paris, 1789). Ironically, phlogiston theorists had argued that
phlogiston had "negative weight" to explain this — which is not a satisfying answer.

**PD sources:**
- Stahl, G.E. *Zymotechnica Fundamentalis* (Halle, 1697). PD (author died 1734).
- Lavoisier, A. *Traité Élémentaire de Chimie* (Paris: Cuchet, 1789). Full scan:
  https://archive.org/details/traitlmentaire00lavo (accessed 2026-06-25). PD (author
  died 1794; published 1789). Contains excellent apparatus diagrams (chemical balance,
  combustion vessels). The diagrams are by the engraver Marie-Anne Lavoisier (his wife) —
  also PD.

**Visual direction:** Take a combustion-apparatus diagram from *Traité Élémentaire de
Chimie*, add caption "Phlogiston released →" pointing to where oxygen is actually
consumed. The visual irony is quiet and satisfying.

**Shirt copy options:**
- "Phlogiston is released during combustion. — G.E. Stahl, 1703."
  (with combustion diagram beneath)
- "Phlogistonated air / Dephlogistonated air / Oxygen / (same thing)"
- "φλόξ — fire / λόγος — logic / Result: none of this is real"

## Sub-idea 3: Ptolemaic geocentric model

**The theory:** The Earth sits motionless at the centre of the universe. The Moon, Sun,
and planets orbit Earth in circles (with secondary circles called epicycles to explain
retrograde motion). The fixed stars are on the outermost sphere. The system was elaborated
by Claudius Ptolemy (*Almagest*, c. 150 CE) and made accurate enough to predict planetary
positions to within a degree or two — good enough for navigation and calendar-keeping.

**What killed it (slowly):** Copernicus (*De revolutionibus*, 1543) proposed the heliocentric
alternative. Kepler's elliptical orbits (1609) simplified it further. Galileo's observations
of Jupiter's moons (1610) showed bodies orbiting something other than Earth. Newton's
gravitation (1687) gave a physical reason for heliocentrism. The Ptolemaic model was not
so much refuted by one experiment as replaced by a simpler and more powerful framework.

**PD source — visually spectacular:**
Andreas Cellarius, *Harmonia Macrocosmica* (Amsterdam: Jan Janssonius, 1660/1661).
This is an atlas of 29 celestial maps engraved in copper, each hand-coloured in the
finest copies. The "Planisphaerii Ptolemaici Scenographia" (Plate 3) shows the full
Ptolemaic system: Earth at centre, concentric circles for Moon / Mercury / Venus / Sun /
Mars / Jupiter / Saturn / Firmament, with the zodiac on the outer ring and cherubs and
allegorical figures in the corners. It is genuinely beautiful.

Wikimedia Commons category: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Harmonia_Macrocosmica
(accessed 2026-06-25). Tagged PD-old-100. Author died 1665; published 1660.

David Rumsey Map Collection also holds high-resolution scans under free licence.

**License:** PD-old-100, multiple sources. Clean up via ImageMagick; the coloured version
is stunning; the uncoloured engraving is also striking and cleaner for printing.

**Visual direction:** Use the Ptolemaic diagram as a large-format shirt print or poster.
Caption: "Planisphaerii Ptolemaici — A. Cellarius, 1661." For a wittier shirt: add
"(The Earth is not the centre.)" in small type.

**Shirt copy options:**
- Full diagram, minimal caption: "Harmonia Macrocosmica — A. Cellarius, 1661"
- "It made perfect sense at the time." (the diagram speaks for itself)
- "Where is the Earth? — Here. — And the Sun? — Also here."

## Sub-idea 4: Caloric theory

**The theory:** Heat is a weightless, self-repelling fluid called *caloric* that flows
from hot bodies to cold ones. Hot objects contain more caloric; cold objects less. The
theory explained heat flow, latent heat, and thermal expansion in an elegantly simple
way. Proposed within the same framework as Lavoisier's chemical revolution — he included
caloric as one of the elements in *Traité Élémentaire de Chimie* (1789).

**What killed it:** Benjamin Thompson, Count Rumford, noticed in 1798 that boring cannon
barrels with a blunt borer generated heat indefinitely, with no change in the metal's
weight. If caloric were a substance, it should have been exhausted. He concluded heat was
a form of motion. Published: "An Inquiry concerning the Source of the Heat which is
excited by Friction," *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society* 88 (1798),
pp. 80–102. James Joule's mechanical equivalent of heat experiment (1843) quantified it:
1 calorie = 4.184 joules. Caloric theory was dead by ~1850.

**PD sources:**
- Lavoisier, *Traité Élémentaire de Chimie* (1789) — includes caloric in the element table
  and heat-flow diagrams. Same scan as Sub-idea 2. PD.
- Rumford, B.T. "An Inquiry concerning the Source of the Heat which is excited by Friction."
  *Phil. Trans. R. Soc.* 88 (1798), pp. 80–102.
  https://archive.org/details/philosophicaltran88royarich (accessed 2026-06-25). PD.

**Visual direction:** A heat-flow diagram (arrows from hot to cold, labelled "caloric")
from the Lavoisier era, with the caption that caloric does not exist. Or: the Rumford
cannon-boring diagram with "Result: heat has no mass" as the punchline.

**Shirt copy options:**
- "Caloric fluid flows from hot to cold. — A.-L. Lavoisier, 1789." (diagram below)
- "Caloric: Q / Kinetic theory: Q = ½mv² / Same thing, different story."
- "An element of fire. (Not an element.)"

## Sub-idea 5: Spontaneous generation

**The theory:** Living organisms can arise from non-living matter. Maggots appear in
rotting meat; mice emerge from grain stores; life originates from mud after floods.
Described by Aristotle (*History of Animals*, c. 343 BCE), assumed to be self-evident for
2,000 years. Even early microscopists (van Leeuwenhoek, 1670s) were uncertain whether
microorganisms arose spontaneously.

**The progressive refutations:**
- Francesco Redi (1668): Flies do not appear in sealed jars of meat — they come from
  eggs laid by other flies. Published: *Esperienze intorno alla generazione degl'insetti*
  (1668). PD (author died 1697).
- Lazzaro Spallanzani (1768): Boiled, sealed broth does not go cloudy — microorganisms
  came from the air. Critics said boiling "killed the vital force."
- Louis Pasteur (1859–1861): Swan-neck flask experiment. Broth in a flask with a long
  S-curved glass neck, boiled and left open to air — no growth (microbes trapped in neck).
  Break the neck: growth within hours. The vital force argument was dead. Published:
  *Mémoire sur les corpuscules organisés qui existent dans l'atmosphère*, in *Annales
  des Sciences Naturelles, Zoologie*, 4ème série, t. 16 (1861). PD (author died 1895;
  published 1861).

**The swan-neck flask** is one of the most recognisable scientific apparatus images.
Pasteur's original drawings are in the 1861 paper. The Musée Pasteur in Paris holds
the original flasks; photos are a separate matter, but the 1861 paper diagrams are PD.

**PD source:**
- Pasteur, L. *Mémoire sur les corpuscules organisés…* (1861). Scans of the original
  journal volume are available via the Bibliothèque nationale de France (Gallica):
  https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/bpt6k64924424 (accessed 2026-06-25). PD.
- Redi, F. *Esperienze intorno alla generazione degl'insetti* (Florence: All'insegna della
  Stella, 1668). https://archive.org/details/esperienzeintor00redigoog (accessed 2026-06-25).
  PD.

**Visual direction:** The swan-neck flask is the hero image — clean line art redrawing
of Pasteur's diagram (simple enough to reconstruct in ~20 lines of Matplotlib). The
straight-necked control flask beside it. Caption: "The vital force is in the neck."

**Shirt copy options:**
- "Life arises spontaneously from non-living matter." [flask diagram] "(It does not.)"
- "La génération spontanée — Réfutée, 1859." (French, which fits Pasteur)
- "Omne vivum ex vivo. — Pasteur, 1859." ("All life from life" — the conclusion of the experiment)

The Latin motto *omne vivum ex vivo* is the strongest option: it states the current
scientific consensus, attributes it to Pasteur, and is entirely in keeping with the
serious-tone differentiator.

## Recommended launch order

1. **Ptolemaic geocentric model** (Sub-idea 3) — the Cellarius plate is immediately
   spectacular, well known, and needs no further argument; best as a large-format print
   or quality shirt with a full-chest image.
2. **Spontaneous generation** (Sub-idea 5) — the swan-neck flask is the simplest to
   redraw; the *omne vivum ex vivo* motto is elegant; high biological/medical audience.
3. **Luminiferous aether** (Sub-idea 1) — clean interferometer diagram; strong physics
   audience; the null-result punchline is understated and satisfying.
4. **Phlogiston** (Sub-idea 2) — excellent for chemistry audience; the Lavoisier apparatus
   diagrams are beautiful; needs more context to read without a caption.
5. **Caloric** (Sub-idea 4) — strongest as a companion to phlogiston (same era, same
   villain/hero); weaker as a standalone because the caloric diagram is less iconic.
